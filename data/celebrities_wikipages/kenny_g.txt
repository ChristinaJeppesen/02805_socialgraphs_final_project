He came into contact with the saxophone when he heard a performance on ''[[The Ed Sullivan Show]]''.<ref name=\"b&n\" /> He started playing the saxophone, a [[Buffet Crampon]] alto, in 1966 when he was 10 years old.<ref name='KennyGBlog2009'>{{cite web |url=http://kennyg.com/blog/ |title=Some more Q&A |access-date=April 1, 2010 |author=Kenny G |date=June 19, 2009 |work=Kenny G Rhythm and Romance |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100324120833/http://kennyg.com/blog/ |archive-date=March 24, 2010 }}</ref>\n\nKenny G attended Whitworth Elementary School, [[Sharples Junior High School]] (renamed Kurose Middle School), [[Franklin High School (Seattle, Washington)|Franklin High School]], and the [[University of Washington]], all in his home city of Seattle. rules|first=Connie|last=Johnson|date=June 12, 1987|work=Los Angeles Times|access-date=25 October 2021}}</ref>\n\n=== 1980s: Start of solo career, ''Duotones'', and ''Silhouette'' ===\nKenny G signed with [[Arista Records]] as a solo artist in 1982, after label president [[Clive Davis]] heard his rendition of \"[[Dancing Queen]]\" by [[ABBA]].<ref name=\"allmusic\" /> He adopted Kenny G as his stage name because it \"had a nice ring to it\".<ref name=LAT90/> His debut studio album, ''[[Kenny G (album)|Kenny G]]'', was recorded in 1981 with members of the Jeff Lorber Fusion, and released in the following year. The album received warm reviews from critics, and reached No. 10 on the ''Billboard'' Jazz Albums chart. Kenny G followed his debut with ''[[G Force]]'' in January 1983 and ''[[Gravity (Kenny G album)|Gravity]]'' in May 1985, both of which reached [[Music recording sales certification|Platinum]] certification in the US for selling one million copies. During this time, he collaborated with musician, singer, songwriter, and producer [[Kashif (musician)|Kashif]] on many tracks, including the 1985 single \"[[Love on the Rise]]\".\n\nIn 1986, Kenny G entered the most commercially successful period of his career. His fourth solo album, ''[[Duotones]]'', was released in September 1986 and features an original instrumental track, \"[[Songbird (Kenny G composition)|Songbird]]\", inspired by his decision to move from Seattle to Los Angeles, which marked the start of a new life for him.<ref name=LAT87/> The album went on to sell five million copies in the US alone and increased his profile worldwide as a result. \"Songbird\" reached No. 4 on the ''Billboard'' Hot 100 and the lead single, \"[[Don't Make Me Wait for Love]]\", featuring [[Lenny Williams]] on lead vocals, went to No. 15 on the Hot 100 and No. 2 on the ''Billboard'' Adult Contemporary chart in 1987.\n\nKenny G worked in the mid-to-late 1980s with jazz and R&B artists such as [[George Benson]], [[Patti LaBelle]] and [[Aretha Franklin]]. The 1987 hit-single \"[[Love Power (Dionne Warwick song)|Love Power]]\", a [[Dionne Warwick]] duet with [[Jeffrey Osborne]] that featured G as a guest saxophonist, peaked at No. 12 on the Billboard Hot 100, and No. 5 on R&B/Hip-Hop songs.\n\nHis first live album, ''[[Kenny G Live]]'', included popular songs, among which \"Going Home\" achieved great success in the [[People's Republic of China]]. The song \"Theme for ''Dying Young''\", written for that movie, was nominated for the Grammy for [[Grammy Award for Best Pop Instrumental Performance|Best Pop Instrumental Performance]]. Kenny G appears on the soundtrack of ''The Bodyguard'', starring [[Kevin Costner]] and [[Whitney Houston]], performing \"Waiting for You\" and \"Even If My Heart Would Break\". The album included many hits such as \"[[Forever in Love (instrumental)|Forever in Love]]\", the recipient of the [[Grammy Award]] for the [[Grammy Award for Best Instrumental Composition|Best Instrumental Composition]] and which charted in the Billboard Year-End Hot 100. \"[[Sentimental (Kenny G composition)|Sentimental]]\" charted at No. 27 on the Adult Contemporary Chart, and \"[[By the Time This Night Is Over]]\", a collaboration with [[Peabo Bryson]], peaked at No. 25 on the Hot 100.\n\nHis first holiday album, ''[[Miracles: The Holiday Album|Miracles]]'', sold over 13 million copies, making it the most successful Christmas album to date.<ref name=\"bmi\" /> He also performed the \"National Anthem of the United States\" at the [[1994 FIFA World Cup]] closing ceremony at the Rose Bowl in Pasadena, California, on July 17, 1994.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ryov4VI8ys4 |archive-url=https://ghostarchive.org/varchive/youtube/20211221/Ryov4VI8ys4 |archive-date=2021-12-21 |url-status=live|title=The Star-Spangled Banner - Kenny G - 1994 FIFA World Cup closing ceremonies at the Rose Bowl in Pasadena, CA, on Jul. 17 1994 |agency=YouTube |date= August 13, 2008|access-date=March 31, 2016}}{{cbignore}}</ref>\n\n[[File:Kenny_G_1998.jpg|thumb|upright|Kenny G in 1998]]\nKenny G earned a place in the ''[[Guinness Book of World Records]]'' in 1997 for playing the longest note ever recorded on a saxophone. [[Pat Metheny]] found him \"not really an advanced player, even [among pop-oriented sax players of that time],\" adding that, according to his opinion, Kenny G had \"major [[rhythm]]ic problems\" and his \"[[Harmony|harmonic]] and [[melodic]] vocabulary was extremely limited, mostly to [[pentatonic]]-based and [[blues]]-lick derived patterns\". Metheny stated that the \"[[controversy]]\" that has surrounded Kenny G \"among musicians and serious listeners\" is due to the fact that \"he sells an enormous amount of records while not being anywhere near a really great player in relation to the standards that have been set on his instrument over the past sixty or seventy years\".<ref name=\"metheny\">{{cite web|date=October 5, 2000 |url=http://www.jazzoasis.com/methenyonkennyg.htm |title=Pat Metheny on Kenny G|website=Jazz Oasis|access-date=December 6, 2021}}</ref> [[Saxophonist]], [[composer]], and [[bandleader]] [[Branford Marsalis]], stating that he's refusing to \"fight that silly war between jazz and [[smooth jazz]],\" called on G's critics as follows: \"When all these jazz guys get in a tizzy over Kenny G, they need to leave Kenny alone. He's not stealing jazz. The audience he has wouldn't be caught dead at a real jazz concert or club. It's not like some guy says, 'You know, I used to listen to [[Miles Davis|Miles]], [[John Coltrane|Trane]] and [[Ornette Coleman|Ornette]]. And then I heard Kenny G, and I never put on another Miles record.' It's a completely different audience.\"<ref>{{cite news|url=https://lasvegassun.com/news/2009/jul/10/5-reasons-we-really-dont-kenny-g/ |first= Joe |last=Brown|title=5 reasons we really don't like Kenny G |newspaper=[[Las Vegas Sun]]|date=July 10, 2009|access-date=December 6, 2021}}</ref>\n\nKenny G's 1999 [[single (music)|single]] \"[[What a Wonderful World]]\" was particularly criticized for its [[overdubbing]] of [[Louis Armstrong]]'s recording of the same song. Mark Sabbatini found G's cover to be an \"unconscionable act of [[Hearing|auditory]] [[graffiti]]\" over the Armstrong song.<ref>{{cite news |last=Sabbatini  |first=Mark  |date=December 30, 2004|title=Kenny G: At Last - The Duets Album|url=http://www.allaboutjazz.com/php/article.php?id=15999 |work=[[All About Jazz]] |access-date=December 6, 2021}}</ref> ''[[The New York Times]]''' [[Ben Ratliff]] opined that a recording by Armstrong, known especially for [[improvisation]], should not be altered by a musician \"whose range and depth of understanding was already in question.\"<ref name=\"ratliffe\">{{cite news|first=Ben|last= Ratliff|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2000/07/16/arts/music-jazz-can-take-itself-too-seriously.html|url-access=subscription| title=Jazz Can Take Itself Too Seriously|website=[[The New York Times]]|date=July 16, 2000|access-date=December 6, 2021}}</ref> [[Alto saxophone|Alto saxophonist]] [[Charles McPherson (musician)|Charles McPherson]] expressed \"mixed feelings,\" stating that Kenny G's \"audience probably does not know Armstrong or [[Stan Getz|Getz]] or [[Charlie Parker]]. So, if he feels like his audience should be more familiar with those people, I can\u2019t see anything [[aesthetical]]ly wrong with that.\" On the other hand, [[pianist]] [[Fred Hersch]], commenting on the occasion of that [[Cover version|cover]] track, said that Kenny G is \"not a jazz musician.\"<ref>{{cite news |last=Varga  |first=George |date=May 1999 |title=Kenny G: Changes His Tune|url=http://jazztimes.com/articles/20699-kenny-g-changes-his-tune |work=[[JazzTimes]]|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20090930234749/http://jazztimes.com/articles/20699-kenny-g-changes-his-tune |archive-date=September 30, 2009  |access-date=December 6, 2021}}</ref>\n\nCritic Josef Woodard found Kenny G serving \"a noble purpose by offering musicians a [[paradigm]] of what ''not'' to do,\" in a ''[[JazzTimes]]'' poll in which, though, players such as [[Stan Kenton]], [[Maynard Ferguson]], [[Miles Davis]], [[John Coltrane]], and [[Ornette Coleman]] were characterized as \"over rated.\"<ref>{{cite web |first=Mike|last= Joyce|url=http://jazztimes.com/articles/24803-who-s-overrated-who-s-underrated  |date=April 30, 2005 |archive-date=March 20, 2011 |title= Who's overrated? Who's Underrated?|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110320125738/http://jazztimes.com/articles/24803-who-s-overrated-who-s-underrated|website=[[JazzTimes]]|access-date=December 6, 2021}}</ref>\n\nIn 2021, [[HBO Max]] started [[streaming]] [[Film director|director]] [[Penny Lane (filmmaker)|Penny Lane]]'s [[Documentary film|documentary]] ''[[Listening to Kenny G]]'', with the subject of the film not having had any control over its content. Lane stated she chose Kenny Gorelick as her subject because he is \"a musician who is objectively popular, by way of [[record sales]], but is also hated by the 'critical class'.\"<ref>{{Cite web|first=Addie |last=Morfoot|url= https://variety.com/2021/film/news/toronto-kenny-g-1235061399/|title= Penny Lane Tunes Up 'Listening to Kenny G' Doc at Toronto Festival|work=[[Variety (magazine)|Variety]]|date=September 11, 2021|access-date=December 6, 2021}}</ref> [[Richard Brody]], in a ''[[The New Yorker|New Yorker]]'' report, cited Kenny G in the film responding \"cheerful[ly]\" to a question about what he loves about music, with, \"I don\u2019t know if I love music that much,\" and continuing, \"when I listen to music, I think about the musicians and I just think about what it takes to make that music and how much they had to practice.\"<ref name=brody>{{cite news |last=Brody|first=Richard |authorlink= Richard Brody|date=December 4, 2021|title='Listening to Kenny G' Is an Ironic Masterpiece|url=https://www.newyorker.com/culture/the-front-row/listening-to-kenny-g-is-an-ironic-masterpiece|url-access=subscription|magazine=[[The New Yorker|New Yorker]]|access-date=December 6, 2021}}</ref>\n\n== Equipment ==\nKenny G plays the [[Selmer Mark VI]] soprano, alto and tenor saxophones.
