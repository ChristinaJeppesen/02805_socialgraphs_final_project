In the late 1980s, he gained national fame as the host of tabloid infotainment TV show ''[[A Current Affair (American TV program)|A Current Affair]]'', based at [[Fox Broadcasting Company|Fox]]'s New York flagship station [[WNYW]]. In 1991 he co-produced his own show ''[[Maury (TV series)|The Maury Povich Show]]'', which in 1998 was rebranded as ''Maury''.\n\n==Early life and education==\nPovich was born on January 17, 1939, into a [[American Jews|Jewish]] family in [[Washington, D.C.]], the second of three children born to Ethyl (n\u00e9e Friedman) and ''[[Washington Post]]'' sportswriter [[Shirley Povich|Shirley Lewis Povich]].<ref name=\"TV Guide\">{{cite web|title=Maury Povich Biography|url=http://www.tvguide.com/celebrities/maury-povich/bio/195236|publisher=TV Guide|access-date=2008-08-25}}</ref> His paternal grandfather, Nathan Povich, emigrated from Russia to the United States in 1878 at age 12.<ref>{{cite book|last=Povich|first=Shirley|title=All Those Mornings...At the Post: The 20th Century in Sports From Famed Washington Post Columnist Shirley Povich|url=https://archive.org/details/allthosemornings00povi/page/n20/mode/1up?q=russia|page=xvii|isbn=978-1-58648-315-9|place=New York, New York|publisher=Public Affairs|year=2005}}</ref><ref>{{cite web |last1=Mensh |first1=Dorice Povich |title=Story of the Povich Family |url=https://www.bathjewishhistory.org/story-of-the-povich-family |website=Bath Jewish History |access-date=3 August 2020}}</ref> Maury graduated from the [[Landon School]] in 1957,<ref name=\"Landon\">{{cite web |title=The Anthony Edward Kupka '64 Distinguished Alumnus Award |url=http://www.landon.net/page.cfm?p=1156 |work=Newsweek |access-date=December 6, 2010 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100620064825/http://www.landon.net/page.cfm?p=1156 |archive-date=June 20, 2010 }}</ref> and from the [[University of Pennsylvania]] in 1962 with a degree in [[broadcast journalism|television journalism]].\n\n==Career==\n\n===1962\u20131986===\nNot long after graduation, Povich got his first job on Washington radio station [[WOL (AM)|WWDC]], where he did publicity and worked as a reporter. By 1966, he was a news reporter and sportscaster for [[WTTG]], the [[Independent station (North America)|independent station]] owned by [[Metromedia]]. In 1967, he became the original co-host of the station's popular midday talk show, ''Panorama'',<ref name=\"Through the Years\"/> which brought the rising star widespread acclaim and national recognition.<ref>Jo Ann Harris. \"Channel 5's Panorama Team.\" ''The Washington Post'', March 23, 1969, p. TV3.</ref>\n\nSeeking to further his career, Povich left Washington in late 1976 for what would become a series of high-profile, short-lived television jobs, beginning with [[WMAQ-TV]] in [[Chicago]] in January 1977.<ref name=\"Gildea\">Gildea, William. \"Povich Off to Chicago.\" ''The Washington Post'', December 8, 1976, p. B1.</ref> Hired to co-anchor evening newscasts, he joined the [[NBC]]-[[Owned and operated station|owned]] station to much fanfare\u2014ahead of his arrival, WMAQ-TV produced promotions featuring testimonials from U.S. senator [[Hubert Humphrey]] and actress [[Carol Channing]], among others, touting Povich's credentials.<ref>{{cite news |last=Deeb |first=Gary |date=January 3, 1977 with a return home to Washington in June 1983, resuming as host of ''Panorama'' and anchoring ''The 10 O'Clock News'' for WTTG.\n\n===1986\u20131990: ''A Current Affair''===\nWhen [[Australia]]n [[media mogul]] [[Rupert Murdoch]] and [[20th Century Fox]] acquired WTTG and the rest of Metromedia's television station group in 1986, one of the first moves made by the newly christened [[Fox Television Stations]] was to bring Povich to [[New York City|New York]] to host ''[[A Current Affair (American TV program)|A Current Affair]]''. Initially launching on [[WNYW]] in July 1986 before landing on the other [[Fox Broadcasting Company|Fox]]-owned stations, and into national syndication in 1988, ''A Current Affair'' was considered a [[Tabloid (newspaper format)|tabloid]] [[infotainment]] show that often focused on celebrity gossip, it also made time for compelling human-interest stories. Critics praised the show for trying to be both informative and entertaining, much like \"a good afternoon newspaper.\"<ref>Corry, John. \"[https://select.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=F50715FE3E5E0C738EDDA10894DE484D81 A Current Affair: Tabloid Journalism]\". ''The New York Times'', August 20, 1986, p. C22.</ref> Povich hosted ''Affair'' until 1990.<ref name=\"Through the Years\"/> While at WNYW, Povich also anchored the station's 7:00&nbsp;p.m. newscasts. He also served two consecutive terms as president of the New York chapter of the [[National Academy of Television Arts and Sciences]].<ref name=\"KTLA\">{{cite web|title=About the Show's Host |url=http://ktla.trb.com/entertainment/syn/maurypovich/ |publisher=KTLA |access-date=2008-08-25 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20080522105240/http://ktla.trb.com/entertainment/syn/maurypovich |archive-date=May 22, 2008 }}</ref>\n\n===1991\u20131998: ''The Maury Povich Show''===\nIn September 1991, he began to host ''[[Maury (TV series)|The Maury Povich Show]]'', which was nationally syndicated and distributed by [[Paramount Domestic Television]] in partnership with his own production company, MoPo Productions, from 1991 to 1998.<ref name=\"Through the Years\"/> For raising awareness of National Adoption Month, Povich was honored by New York City mayor [[Rudolph Giuliani]] in 1995.\n\n===1998\u20132022: ''Maury'', ''Twenty One'', and ''Weekends with Maury and Connie''===\nIn 1998, the show was taken over by Studios USA (then a division of [[USA Network]]s, later renamed Universal Television after being sold to [[Vivendi]] Universal; and [[NBCUniversal Television Distribution]] after VU Entertainment was sold to [[NBC]] owner [[General Electric]]). When Studios USA took over production of the show, it was renamed simply ''Maury''. The show often veered into what critics called trash TV, and in 1998 it became known for a segment called \"Who's the Daddy?\" during which men who were denying or trying to establish paternity were given DNA tests and the results were revealed on the air.<ref name=\"Through the Years\"/>\n\nOn January 9, 2000, Povich hosted the short-lived primetime revival of the classic [[game show]] ''[[Twenty One (game show)|Twenty One]]'' on NBC. Reruns of the show have been aired on [[Game Show Network|GSN]].\n\nIn November 2005, [[MSNBC]] announced Povich would co-host a weekend [[news program]] with his wife, Connie Chung. The program titled ''[[Weekends with Maury and Connie]]'' debuted on January 7, 2006, but was canceled due to low ratings. The final episode aired on June 17, 2006.<ref>{{cite news |url=http://www.mediabistro.com/tvnewser/msnbc/exclusive_msnbc_axes_maury_connie_38278.asp |publisher=Media Bistro |work=TV Newser |title=MSNBC Axes ''Maury & Connie'' |date=June 9, 2006 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20060619202905/http://www.mediabistro.com/tvnewser/msnbc/exclusive_msnbc_axes_maury_connie_38278.asp |archive-date=June 19, 2006 }}</ref>\n\nHe appeared as himself in the fourth episode of the [[How I Met Your Mother (season 6)|sixth season]] of the sitcom ''[[How I Met Your Mother]]''. He is portrayed as a New Yorker who is apparently seen everywhere in the city\u2014often in several places at once. He also appeared as himself in the film ''[[Madea's Big Happy Family (film)|Madea's Big Happy Family]]''.\n\nIn May 2007, he launched the ''[[Flathead Beacon]]'', a weekly print newspaper and online news source in Montana's [[Flathead County]], where he has a home and is a member at the Eagle Bend Golf Club.\n\nIn 2015, Povich made a guest appearance on the [[Adult Swim]] show ''[[The Jack and Triumph Show]]''.\n\n==Personal life==\nFrom 1962 to 1979, Povich was married to Phyllis Minkoff. Maury and Phyllis have two daughters together, Susan Anne and Amy Joyce Povich. Their daughter Amy is married to physician and author [[David Agus]].{{Citation needed|date=February 2018}}\n\nIn 1984, he married [[news anchor]] [[Connie Chung]], whom he had met while working in the news department at WTTG.<ref>{{cite news |title=Connie Chung Offers Personal View of Maury Povich |author=Allison J. Povich took a journalistic approach to each story and took pride when an appearance on ''Maury'' would lead to the father and mother of a child reconciling.<ref name=mauryspostmortem>{{Cite web |last=Braxton |first=Greg |date=2022-06-22 |title=Maury Povich, king of daytime, takes a final bow |url=https://www.latimes.com/entertainment-arts/tv/story/2022-06-22/maury-povich-exit-interview |access-date=2022-06-22 |website=Los Angeles Times |language=en-US}}</ref>\n\nIn 2017, Povich became an investor and part owner of the Washington DC bar and restaurant Chatter, along with other DC notables [[Tony Kornheiser]] and [[Gary Williams]].<ref>Steinberg, Dan (April 24, 2017)|url=https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/dc-sports-bog/wp/2017/04/24/tony-kornheisers-d-c-bar-finally-has-its-new-name/</ref>\n\nHe is a fan of [[University of Central Florida]] football.\n\nPovich owns a ranch just outside of [[Bigfork, Montana]], where he lives several months of the year.\n\nMaury is an avid golfer and has been playing golf since he was a child. He is a frequent player at [[Pebble Beach Golf Links|Pebble Beach]], [[Torrey Pines Golf Course|Torrey Pines]], [[Farm Neck Golf Club|Farm Neck]], and [[Old Course]] in [[Scotland]]. He still plays in amateur senior tournaments with a 2.4 handicap and in 2007 was placed in ''[[Golf Digest]]''{{'s}} top 100 celebrity golfers. In 2000, Maury participated in the U.S. Senior Amateur championship where he lost in the first round of the match play competition.
