The company operates under a number of [[subsidiary]] [[brand name]]s, including the flagship '''Chiquita''' brand and Fresh Express salads.  Chiquita is the leading distributor of bananas in the United States.\n\nChiquita is the successor to the [[United Fruit Company]].  It was formerly controlled by American businessman [[Carl Lindner, Jr.|Carl H. Lindner, Jr.]], whose majority ownership of the company ended when Chiquita Brands International exited a prepackaged Chapter 11 bankruptcy on 19 March 2002.  In 2003, the company acquired the German produce distribution company, [[Atlanta AG]]. Fresh Express salads was purchased from Performance Food Group in 2005. In 1873 Central American railroad developer [[Minor C. Keith]] began to experiment with banana production in Costa Rica.  Later, he planted bananas alongside a Costa Rican railroad track to provide revenue for the railroad.<ref name=history>{{cite web|title=The Chiquita Story|publisher=Chiquita Brands International|url=http://www.chiquita.com/Our-Company/The-Chiquita-Story.aspx|access-date=13 March 2014|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140310203525/http://www.chiquita.com/Our-Company/The-Chiquita-Story.aspx|archive-date=10 March 2014}}</ref> In 1878, Baker partnered with [[Andrew Preston]] to form the [[Boston Fruit Company]].<ref>{{cite book |first=Dan |last=Koeppel |title=Banana: The Fate of the Fruit that Changed the World |location=New York |publisher=Hudson Press |year=2008 |pages=[https://archive.org/details/bananafateoffrui00koep/page/52 52\u201356] |isbn=978-1-59463-038-5 |url=https://archive.org/details/bananafateoffrui00koep/page/52 }}</ref>\n\n[[United Fruit Company]] was founded in 1899 when the Boston Fruit Company and various fruit exporting concerns controlled by Keith merged. In 1903, United Fruit Company was listed on the New York Stock Exchange and became the first company to use refrigeration during open sea transport.\n\nIn 1928, workers went on strike in protest against poor pay and working conditions in the company plantations [[Ci\u00e9naga, Magdalena|Ci\u00e9naga]] ([[Colombia]]). The company lobbied U.S. government forces to assist with repressing the outbreak; however, the Colombian government opted to quell the strike on its own, sending military forces into the town of Ci\u00e9naga, where the strikers had gathered, on 6 December. The repression resulted in the deaths of scores of plantation workers and their families.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.historychannel.com.au/this-day-in-history/banana-massacre/|title=Banana Massacre &#124; History Channel on Foxtel|date=19 June 2016|website=History Channel}}</ref> This episode is known in the history of Colombia as the Masacre de las Bananeras ([[Banana massacre]]). [[Gabriel Garc\u00eda M\u00e1rquez]] alludes to the event in his novel [[One Hundred Years of Solitude]] by describing a military suppression that resulted in the death of 3,000 plantation workers in the fictional town of Macondo. While Garc\u00eda M\u00e1rquez has stated that the deaths in his novel are potential overestimations, the actual number of deaths has never been confirmed. Estimates gathered from oral histories to primary sources vary widely, from 47 to upwards of 1,000 casualties.<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.colombiainforma.info/la-masacre-de-las-bananeras-en-cien-anos-de-soledad-cuando-el-realismo-magico-le-gano-a-la-historia-oficial/ |title=La Masacre de las bananeras en cien anos de soledad cuando el realismo magico le gano a la historia oficial |website=colombiainforma.info |date=2015-05-12 |access-date=2018-04-16 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20161201164759/http://www.colombiainforma.info/la-masacre-de-las-bananeras-en-cien-anos-de-soledad-cuando-el-realismo-magico-le-gano-a-la-historia-oficial |archive-date=1 December 2016 |url-status=dead }}</ref>\n\nBy 1930, the company's fleet had grown to 95 ships.<ref name=history />\n\nIn 1944, the company premiered the \"Chiquita Banana\" advertising jingle, which extolled the virtues of the fruit as well as when to eat them and how to store them. The section accused the company of mistreating workers on its [[Central American]] [[banana plantation|plantations]], polluting the environment, allowing [[cocaine]] to be brought to Borneo on its ships, bribing foreign officials, evading foreign nations' laws on land ownership, forcibly preventing its workers from unionizing, and a host of other misdeeds.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.mindfully.org/Pesticide/chiquita/|title=Chiquita SECRETS Revealed|author=mindfully.org|website=Mindfully.org|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20031104072207/http://www.mindfully.org/Pesticide/chiquita/|archive-date=4 November 2003|url-status=dead|access-date=2016-07-28}}</ref> Chiquita denied all the allegations, and sued after it was revealed that Gallagher had repeatedly hacked into Chiquita's [[voicemail]] system.<ref name=\"Cincinnati Vaccariello\">{{cite magazine|title=The rise and fall of an editor, a reporter, and a newspaper|first1=Linda|last1=Vaccariello|first2=Skip|last2=Tate|magazine=[[Cincinnati (magazine)|Cincinnati]]|publisher=Emmis Communications|volume=33|issue=1|date=October 1998|pages=112\u2013114, 153\u2013168|issn=0746-8210|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=7u0CAAAAMBAJ&pg=PA112|via=Google Books}}</ref> A special prosecutor was appointed to investigate, because the elected prosecutor at the time had ties to [[Carl Lindner, Jr.]]\n\nGallagher had claimed to have obtained over 2,000 voicemails from a Chiquita executive, but in truth he had obtained them by hacking into Chiquita's voicemail system as often as 35 times a day. He had continued hacking into the system despite being explicitly directed not to do so by editors and lawyers. According to McWhirter, he also refused to give straight answers about his source to editors and outside lawyers\u2013facts that aroused the suspicions of McWhirter and other reporters.<ref name=\"Cincinnati Vaccariello\"/><ref name=\"CJR McWhirter\">{{cite magazine|title=Saved by the Shield|first=Cameron|last=McWhirter|magazine=[[Columbia Journalism Review]]|publisher=Columbia University Graduate School of Journalism|date=May\u2013June 2008|url=https://www.cjr.org/feature/saved_by_the_shield.php}}</ref><ref name=\"NYT Frantz\">{{cite news|title=Chiquita Still Under Cloud After Newspaper's Retreat|first=Douglas|last=Frantz|work=The New York Times|date=July 17, 1998|url=https://www.nytimes.com/1998/07/17/us/chiquita-still-under-cloud-after-newspaper-s-retreat.html}}</ref>\n\nSix weeks after the stories ran, Gannett reached a settlement with Chiquita, averting a lawsuit. Chiquita Brands International}}[[File:Chiquita_Papers_Document.jpg|thumb|right|One of several documents obtained by the National Security Archive related to Chiquita's payments to terrorist groups in Colombia]]\n\nIn the 1990s and early 2000s, faced with an unstable political situation in Colombia, Chiquita and several other corporations including the [[Dole Food Company]], [[Fresh Del Monte Produce]] and [[Hyundai Motor Corporation]] made payments to paramilitary organizations in the country, most notably the [[United Self-Defense Forces of Colombia]] (AUC).<ref name=\"pulitzercenter\">{{cite web|url=https://pulitzercenter.org/reporting/chiquita-made-killing-colombias-civil-war|title=Chiquita Made a Killing From Colombia's Civil War|first=Matt|last=Kennard|website=[[Pulitzer Center]]|date=27 January 2017|access-date=21 May 2020}}</ref> Chiquita paid the AUC $1.7 million in a ten-year period. Although official accounts from the company state they only made these payments as the AUC was extorting payments from Chiquita in order to ensure their security,<ref name=\"latimes.com\">|{{cite web|url=https://articles.latimes.com/2007/mar/15/world/fg-chiquita15|title=Chiquita to pay fine for deals with militants|work=latimes.com|date=15 March 2007|access-date=21 May 2020}}</ref> these claims are disputed as Chiquita also allowed AUC to use their loading facilities to transport [[AK-47]]s.<ref name=\"newrepublic\">{{cite web|url=https://newrepublic.com/article/121506/supreme-court-should-take-chiquitas-terrorism-sponsorship-case|title=The Supreme Court Needs to Decide: Can Victims Sue Chiquita For Sponsoring Terrorism?|first=Steven|last=Cohen|website=[[The New Republic]]|date=10 April 2015|access-date=21 May 2020}}</ref> Indeed, the Chiquita's United States counsel had warned them against using this extortion defense in cases where the company benefitted from these payments,<ref name=\"gwu\">{{cite web|url=https://nsarchive2.gwu.edu//NSAEBB/NSAEBB340/index.htm|date=7 April 2011|access-date=21 May 2020|title=The Chiquita Papers|first=Michael|last=Evans}}</ref> and the company's lawyer reportedly told them to stop making the payments.<ref name=\"newrepublic\"/> Chiquita's dealings with AUC continued even after it was officially designated as a terrorist organization in the United States. Although the company eventually voluntarily disclosed their involvement with AUC to the [[United States Department of Justice]],<ref name=\"cnnmoney\">{{cite web|url=https://money.cnn.com/2007/03/14/news/companies/chiquita/index.htm|title=Chiquita to plead guilty to ties with terrorists|date=14 March 2007|access-date=21 May 2020}}</ref> they still sent over $300,000 to the organization even after the Justice Department instructed them to halt all payments.<ref name=\"newrepublic\"/>\n\nOn 14 March 2007, Chiquita Brands was fined $25 million as part of a settlement with the [[United States Justice Department]] for having ties to [[paramilitarism in Colombia|Colombian paramilitary]] groups.  According to court documents, between 1997 and 2004, officers of a Chiquita subsidiary paid approximately $1.7 million to the right-wing [[United Self-Defense Forces of Colombia]] (AUC), in exchange for local employee protection in Colombia's volatile banana harvesting zone. Similar payments were also made to the [[Revolutionary Armed Forces of Colombia]] (FARC), as well as the [[National Liberation Army (Colombia)|National Liberation Army]] (ELN) from 1989 to 1997, both left-wing organizations.<ref name=\"Evans\">Michael Evans, [http://www.thenation.com/article/para-politics-goes-bananas 'Para-politics' Goes Bananas], ''[[The Nation]]'', 4 April 2007</ref><ref name=\"Apuzzo\">Matt Apuzzo, [[Associated Press]] writer, [http://www.abcmoney.co.uk/news/15200739657.htm Chiquita to Pay $25M Fine in Terror Case] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110131052521/http://www.abcmoney.co.uk/news/15200739657.htm|date=31 January 2011}}, ''[[ABC News]]'', 15 March 2007</ref> All three of these groups are on the U.S. State Department's list of [[Foreign Terrorist Organizations]]. Their strategy was to encourage [[History of peasant banana production in the Americas|small farming of bananas]] rather than [[History of modern banana plantations in the Americas|large scale monoculture]], and to push for subsidies and other government relief to level the field for small producers.  The [[fair trade movement]], which sought to influence consumers to purchase the products of smallholders, also joined in the action.<ref name=\"small\">{{cite book|title=Smart Alliance: How Global Capitalism and Environmental Activists Transformed a Tarnished Brand|last1=Taylor|first1=J. The changes focused on the use of pesticides but also affected corporate culture.<ref name=\"small\" /> In 2000, Chiquita adopted a new code of conduct that included [[Social Accountability International]]'s [[SA8000]] labor standard.  Also in 2000, Chiquita achieved Rainforest Alliance certification for environmentally friendly practices on 100% of its farms.
