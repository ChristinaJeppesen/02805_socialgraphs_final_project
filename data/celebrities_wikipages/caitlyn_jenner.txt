This prompted the ''[[Eugene Register-Guard]]'' to ask: \"Who's Jenner?\"<ref name=\"zarnowski 1992\"/><ref name=\"usot\"/><ref name=tjetwn/> Following the trials, Jenner was tenth in the [[Athletics at the 1972 Summer Olympics \u2013 Men's decathlon|decathlon]] at the [[1972 Summer Olympics]] in [[Munich]], [[West Germany]].<ref name=\"munchen summer\"/> By&nbsp;watching [[Soviet Union at the 1972 Summer Olympics#Athletics|Soviet]] [[Mykola Avilov]] win the event, Jenner was inspired to start an intense training regimen. \"For the first time, I knew what I wanted out of life and that was it, and this guy has it. I literally started training that night at midnight, running through the streets of Munich, <!--West-->Germany, training for the Games. I trained that day on through the [[Athletics at the 1976 Summer Olympics \u2013 Men's decathlon|1976 Games]], 6\u20138 hours a day, every day, 365 days a year.\"<ref name=\"espnoly\"/>\n\nAfter graduating from Graceland, Jenner married girlfriend Chrystie Crownover and moved to [[San Jose, California]]. Crownover provided most of the family income as a flight attendant for [[United Airlines]].<ref name=\"forgotten history\"/> Jenner trained during the day and sold insurance at night, earning {{US$|9000|link=yes}} a year.<ref name=\"OK!\"/><ref name=\"ABC42415\"/> In the era before [[Amateur sports#Olympics|professional athletes]] were allowed to compete in Olympic sports, this kind of training was unheard of. On the other hand, Soviet athletes were state sponsored, which gave them an advantage over amateur American athletes.<ref name=\"auto\">{{cite web|url=http://blogs.bu.edu/guidedhistory/russia-and-its-empires/tyler-benson/|title=The Role of Sports in The Soviet Union - Guided History|website=blogs.bu.edu}}</ref><ref name=\"cia.gov\">{{cite web|url=https://www.cia.gov/library/readingroom/docs/CIA-RDP80-00810A005600130009-0.pdf |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170123110037/https://www.cia.gov/library/readingroom/docs/CIA-RDP80-00810A005600130009-0.pdf |url-status=dead |archive-date=January 23, 2017 |title=Info |website=www.cia.gov }}</ref> During this period, Jenner trained at the [[San Jose City College]] (SJCC) and [[San Jose State University]] (SJSU) tracks.<ref name=\"speed\"/><ref name=\"decathlon hopeful\"/> San Jose athletics centered on SJCC coach [[Bert Bonanno]]; at that time, the city was a hotbed for training and was called the \"Track Capital of the World\".<ref name=\"ABC42415\"/><!-- Note: slogan on Jenner's shirt at 4:47 in part II of the video --> Many other aspiring Olympic athletes also trained at San Jose; the list included [[Millard Hampton]], [[Andre Phillips]], [[John Powell (discus thrower)|John Powell]], [[Mac Wilkins]], and [[Al Feuerbach]].<ref name=\"speed\"/><ref name=\"bj wins decathlon\"/> Jenner's best events were on day two of the decathlon: hurdles, discus, pole vault, javelin, and 1500 meters.<!-- the [[decathlon]], is a two day event with 5 events each day. During the [[Cold War]] in 1972, three major Olympic titles that had a long history of American success{{snds}}[[Basketball at the 1972 Summer Olympics|basketball]], [[Athletics at the 1972 Summer Olympics \u2013 Men's 100 metres|the 100 meter dash]], and [[Athletics at the 1972 Summer Olympics{{snds}}Men's decathlon|decathlon]]{{snds}}were won by [[Soviet Union|Soviet]] athletes. All Soviet athletes were professionals, while the United States was limited to amateurs.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://blogs.bu.edu/guidedhistory/russia-and-its-empires/tyler-benson/|title=The Role of Sports in The Soviet Union - Guided History}}</ref><ref name=\"auto\"/><ref name=\"cia.gov\"/> Jenner became an American hero by returning the decathlon title to the United States. \"After the Games were over,\" Jenner said, \"I happened to be the right guy, at that right place, at that right time.\"<ref name=\"espnoly\"/> [[Tony Kornheiser]] of ''[[The New York Times]]'' wrote that along with wife, Chrystie, Jenner was \"so high up on the pedestal of American heroism, it would take a crane to get them down.\"<ref name=\"dmr\"/><ref name=\"forgotten history\"/><ref name=\"spotlight shifts\"/>\n\nAfter the expected Olympic success, Jenner planned to cash in on whatever celebrity status could follow a gold medal in the same mold as [[Johnny Weissmuller]] and [[Sonja Henie]], who had become major movie stars following their gold medals. This would require forgoing any future Olympic competition. At the time, Jenner's agent George Wallach felt there was a four-year window{{snds}}until the next Olympics{{snds}}upon which to capitalize. Wallach reported that Jenner was being considered for the role of ''[[Superman (1978 film)|Superman]]'', which ultimately went to [[Christopher Reeve]]. \"I really don't know how many offers we have\", Wallach claimed. \"There are still unopened telegrams back at the hotel and you just can't believe the offers that poured in during the first two days.\"<ref name=\"nydn\"/>\n\nJenner appeared on the cover of the August 9, 1976, issue of ''[[Sports Illustrated]]'',<ref name=\"August 9, 1976\"/> the February 1979 issue of ''[[Gentleman's Quarterly]]'',<ref name=\"sexy 1979\"/> and on the cover of ''Playgirl'' magazine.<ref name=\"formerly bruce\"/> Jenner became a spokesperson for [[Tropicana Products|Tropicana]], [[Minolta]], and [[Caleres|Buster Brown shoes]].<ref name=\"espnoly\"/> Jenner was also selected by the [[Sacramento Kings|Kansas City Kings]] with the 139th overall pick in the seventh round of the [[1977 NBA draft]] despite not having played basketball since high school.<ref>[http://www.prosportstransactions.com/basketball/DraftTrades/Years/1977.htm 1977 NBA Draft \u2013 Pro Sports Transactions.] Retrieved January 15, 2018</ref> The [[publicity stunt]] was executed by team president/general manager [[Joe Axelson]] to mock the [[Kansas City Chiefs]]' yearly claims that they planned on selecting \"the best athlete available\" in the [[National Football League Draft]].
