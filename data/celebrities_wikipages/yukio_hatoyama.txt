      = Yukio Hatoyama signature.svg\n}}\n{{nihongo|'''Yukio Hatoyama'''|\u9ce9\u5c71 \u7531\u7d00\u592b|Hatoyama Yukio|extra=born 11 February 1947}} is a former Japanese politician who served as [[Prime Minister of Japan]] from 16 September 2009 to 8 June 2010. He was the first Prime Minister from the modern [[Democratic Party of Japan]].<ref>[https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB10001424052748704875604575281292285208862?mod=WSJ_hpp_LEFTTopStories Wall Street Journal]</ref>\n\nFirst elected to the [[House of Representatives of Japan|House of Representatives]] in 1986, Hatoyama became [[History of the Democratic Party of Japan#Presidents of DPJ|President of the DPJ]], the main opposition party, in May 2009. He then led the party to victory in the [[2009 Japanese general election|August 2009 general election]], defeating the long-governing [[Liberal Democratic Party of Japan|Liberal Democratic Party]] (LDP), which had been in power for over a decade. He represented the [[Hokkaido's 9th district|Hokkaido 9th district]] in the House of Representatives from 1986 to 2012.\n\n==Early life and family==\n[[File:Ichiro Hatoyama with two grandsons.jpg|thumb|left|200px|[[Ichir\u014d Hatoyama]] and his two grandsons, Yukio and [[Kunio Hatoyama|Kunio]]]]\nHatoyama comes from a prominent Japanese political family which has been likened to the [[Kennedy family]] of the United States.<ref name=afp_wsj>{{cite news|first=Miwa |last=Suzuki |title=Japan's first lady hopeful an outgoing TV lifestyle guru |url=http://www.france24.com/en/20090824-japans-first-lady-hopeful-outgoing-tv-lifestyle-guru |agency=Agence France-Presse |publisher=[[France 24]] |date=24 August 2009 |access-date=31 August 2009 |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100105160748/http://www.france24.com/en/20090824-japans-first-lady-hopeful-outgoing-tv-lifestyle-guru |archive-date=5 January 2010 }}; Hayashi, Yuka. [https://www.wsj.com/articles/SB124908951974798603 \"Japan's Hatoyama Sustains Family Political Tradition,\"] ''Wall Street Journal'' (WSJ). 1 August 2009.</ref>\n\nHatoyama, who was born in [[Bunky\u014d]], Tokyo, is a fourth-generation politician. His paternal great-grandfather, [[Kazuo Hatoyama]], was speaker of the House of Representatives of the [[Diet of Japan]] from 1896 to 1897 during the [[Meiji era]].<ref name=\"asiatimesbackground\">Takahashi, Kosuke. [https://web.archive.org/web/20090830092207/http://www.atimes.com/atimes/Japan/KH29Dh01.html \"Japan on the brink of a new era\"], ''[[Asia Times]]'', 29 August 2009.</ref> Kazuo later served as the president of [[Waseda University]].<ref name=\"asiatimesbackground\"/> His paternal great-grandmother, [[Haruko Hatoyama]], was a co-founder of what is known today as [[Kyoritsu Women's University]]. His paternal grandfather, [[Ichir\u014d Hatoyama]], was a major politician; he served as Prime Minister and was a founder and the first [[History of the Liberal Democratic Party (Japan)#Presidents of the LDP|President of the Liberal Democratic Party]] in 1956. As Prime Minister, he restored [[diplomatic relations]] with the [[Soviet Union]], which cleared the way for Japan's membership in the United Nations.<ref name=\"asiatimesbackground\"/>\n\nHatoyama is the son of [[Iichir\u014d Hatoyama]], who was [[Minister for Foreign Affairs (Japan)|Foreign Minister]] for a time. His mother, [[Yasuko Hatoyama]], is a daughter of [[Shojiro Ishibashi]], the founder of [[Bridgestone Corporation]] and [[heir]] to his significant [[inheritance]].<ref name=afp_wsj/> Yasuko Hatoyama is known as the \"Godmother\" within the Japanese political world for her financial contributions to both of her sons' political ambitions.<ref name=\"asiatimesbackground\"/> In particular, Yasuko donated billions of yen when Kunio and Yukio co-created their [[Democratic Party of Japan (1996-1998)|previous Democratic Party of Japan]] (DPJ) in 1996 to help establish her sons' fledgling political party.<ref name=\"asiatimesbackground\"/>\n\nHis younger brother, [[Kunio Hatoyama]], served as [[Ministry of Internal Affairs and Communications|Minister of Internal Affairs and Communications]] under Prime Minister [[Taro Aso]] until 12 June 2009. His younger sister-in-law {{nihongo|[[Emily Hatoyama]]|[[:ja:\u9ce9\u5c71\u30a8\u30df\u30ea|\u9ce9\u5c71\u30a8\u30df\u30ea]]}} who is Kunio's wife, an Australian Japanese, was a TV personality in Japan.\n\nHatoyama graduated with a [[Bachelor of Engineering]] from the [[University of Tokyo]] in 1969 and received a PhD in [[industrial engineering|Industrial Engineering]] from [[Stanford University]] in 1976.<ref name=\"dpj.or.jp\">{{cite web |title=Yukio Hatoyama |url=http://www.dpj.or.jp/english/about_us/sec_gen.html |archive-url=https://archive.today/20070807191659/http://www.dpj.or.jp/english/about_us/sec_gen.html |url-status=dead |archive-date=7 August 2007 |work=The Democratic Party of Japan }}</ref> He met his wife, [[Miyuki Hatoyama]], while studying at Stanford (Miyuki was working at a Japanese restaurant).<ref name=\"asiatimesbackground\"/> The couple married in 1975 after Miyuki divorced her ex-husband.<ref name=afp_wsj/> The couple's son, {{nihongo|[[Kiichir\u014d Hatoyama|Kiichir\u014d]]|[[:ja:\u9ce9\u5c71\u7d00\u4e00\u90ce|\u7d00\u4e00\u90ce]]}}, graduated from the [[urban engineering]] department of the [[University of Tokyo]], is a visiting engineering researcher at [[Moscow State University]].<ref name=\"asiatimesbackground\"/>\n\nHatoyama worked as assistant professor (1976\u20131981) at [[Tokyo Institute of Technology]] and later transferred to [[Senshu University]] as associate professor (1981\u20131984).\n\nHatoyama's son [[Kiichiro Hatoyama]] (1976) is married and has given Hatoyama senior two grandchildren.\n\n=== Ancestry ===\n{{ahnentafel\n|collapsed=yes |align=center\n|boxstyle_1=background-color: #fcc;\n|boxstyle_2=background-color: #fb9;\n|boxstyle_3=background-color: #ffc;\n|boxstyle_4=background-color: #bfc;\n|boxstyle_5=background-color: #9fe;\n|1= 1. Ishibashi Matsu\n}}\n\n==Political career==\n[[File:Vladimir Putin in Japan 3-5 September 2000-17.jpg|thumb|200px|with [[Vladimir Putin]] (5 September 2000)]]\nHatoyama ran for a seat in the [[Hokkaido's 9th district|Hokkaido 9th District]] and was elected to the House of Representatives in 1986 representing the ruling LDP. In 1993 he left the LDP to form the [[New Party Sakigake]] with [[Naoto Kan]], [[Masayoshi Takemura]] and {{nihongo|[[Sh\u016bsei Tanaka]]|[[:ja:\u7530\u4e2d\u79c0\u5f81|\u7530\u4e2d\u79c0\u5f81]]}}. He and Kan then left to join the newly formed Democratic Party of Japan (1996).\n\nHatoyama and his younger brother, Kunio Hatoyama, co-created the party, using billions of yen donated by their mother, Yasuko.<ref name=\"asiatimesbackground\"/> Kunio Hatoyama eventually left the DPJ, saying the party had drifted too far to the left from its original centrist roots, and rejoined the [[Liberal Democratic Party (Japan)|Liberal Democratic Party]] (LDP).<ref name=\"asiatimesbackground\"/> Yukio remained with the party through its merger with several other opposition parties in 1998.\n\nThe elder Hatoyama became the Democratic Party of Japan's chairman and leader of the opposition from 1999 to 2002, after which he resigned to take responsibility for the confusion that arose from rumors of mergers with [[Ichir\u014d Ozawa]]'s then [[Liberal Party (Japan, 1998)|Liberal Party]]. He was Secretary-General of the Democratic Party of Japan (DPJ)<ref name=\"dpj.or.jp\"/> before he succeeded Ozawa as party leader following Ozawa's resignation on 11 May 2009. Hatoyama was chosen by fellow party representatives on 16 May 2009, winning 124 of the 219 votes and defeating rival [[Katsuya Okada]].<ref>{{cite news|title=Hatoyama Wins Election to Head Japan's Biggest Opposition Party|url=https://www.bloomberg.com/apps/news?pid=20601101&sid=aObYYioBhmXg&refer=japan|publisher=Bloomberg News|date=16 May 2009 |access-date=16 May 2009}}</ref>\n\nBecause of his quirky hairstyle, prominent eyes, and eccentric manner, he is known by his supporters and his opposition alike as \"ET\" or \"The Alien\",<ref name=et>{{cite news|title=New Japan PM earned alien name, wife says\n|url=http://www.brisbanetimes.com.au/world/its-in-the-blood-brothers-set-to-be-nations-political-rivals-20090830-f404.html|work=Brisbane Times|date=31 August 2009|access-date=1 September 2009}}</ref> a nickname his wife states he earned because of how different he is from old-style Japanese politicians.<ref name=\"alien\">{{cite news |first = Mark|last = Willacy|title = New Japan PM earned alien name, wife says|url = http://www.abc.net.au/news/stories/2009/09/01/2673604.htm|work = [[ABC News (Australia)|ABC News]]|date = 1 September 2009|access-date = 1 September 2009}}</ref> Another nickname commonly used by the Japanese public in press was Popo, after a children's song about a pigeon that starts with the lyric \"popopo, hato popo\"; the first character in Hatoyama's last name is the Japanese word for 'pigeon'.\n\n==Prime minister==\n[[File:Yukio Hatoyama and Barack Obama cropped Miyuki Hatoyama Michelle Obama Yukio Hatoyama and Barack Obama 20090923.jpg|thumb|200px|with [[Barack Obama]] (at the [[Metropolitan Museum of Art]] on 23 September 2009)]]\n[[File:Robert Gates and Yukio Hatoyama 20090821 1.jpg|thumb|200px|with [[Robert Gates]] (at the [[Prime Minister's Official Residence (Japan)|Prime Minister's Official Residence]] on 21 October 2009)]]\n{{See also|2009 Japanese general election}}\nHatoyama entered his prime minister career with a high approval rating. It was revealed that Hatoyama received $4&nbsp;million in [[donation]]s that were improperly reported. Most of the money was given by his mother, a wealthy heiress, and some of the reported givers had the names of deceased people. The scandal raised questions about his credibility while also highlighting his privileged background.<ref>{{cite news|last=Fackler|first=Martin|title=Doubts Grow in Japan About Premier Amid Money Scandal |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2009/12/19/world/asia/19japan.html|work=The New York Times|date=18 December 2009|access-date=8 January 2010}}</ref> However, according to [[NHK]] in 2010 prosecutors chose not to pursue him citing insufficient evidence of criminal activity, although a secretary was given a suspended prison sentence, and a review panel commented: \"it is difficult to believe Hatoyama's assertion he was unaware of the falsifications.\"<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.nhk.or.jp/daily/english/26_16.html|title=NHK\u30aa\u30f3\u30e9\u30a4\u30f3|publisher=NHK|access-date=2 July 2010|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20110621220521/http://www3.nhk.or.jp/daily/english/26_16.html|archive-date=21 June 2011}}</ref>\n\n===Spending review===\nIn December, the DPJ created a government task force to review government spending and pledged to make cuts equal to $32.8&nbsp;billion. In order not to anger Washington, Hatoyama offered $5&nbsp;billion in civilian aid for Afghanistan reconstruction.<ref>{{cite news|last=Fackler|first=Martin |title=Japan Ends Naval Support for Afghan War |url=https://www.nytimes.com/2010/01/16/world/asia/16japan.html?ref=asia|work=The New York Times|date=15 January 2010}}</ref>\n\nHatoyama was also faced with the issue of the relocation of the American [[Marine Corps Air Station Futenma|Futenma Marine Corps Air Base]]. Times|date=13 November 2009|access-date=8 January 2010}}</ref> Some voices in the DPJ demanded that America move its military bases off Okinawa islands altogether.<ref name=\"usties\"/> Hatoyama was torn between public opinion on Okinawa and the desire to retain strong ties with Washington.\n\nIn moving towards a more Asia-centered foreign policy, Hatoyama worked towards making relations better Japan, proposing his vision for a pan-Asian leadership agenda (30571226673).jpg|thumb|200px|Yukio Hatoyama (at the [[Horasis|Horasis Asia Meeting]] in November 2016)]]\nAfter stepping down as prime minister Hatoyama continued to serve as a DPJ diet member. When Prime Minister [[Yoshihiko Noda]] introduced legislation to raise the consumption tax from 5% to 10% Hatoyama was one of 57 DPJ lower house lawmakers who voted against the bill.<ref>The Daily Yomiuri [http://www.yomiuri.co.jp/dy/national/T120627005309.htm Local DPJ chapters blast tax-vote rebel lawmakers June 28 2012]. Retrieved 22 July 2012</ref> His membership in the DPJ was suspended for six months, subsequently reduced to three.<ref>The Japan Times [http://www.japantimes.co.jp/text/nn20120710a3.html Hatoyama's DPJ membership suspension halved 10 July 2012]. Retrieved 22 July 2012</ref>\n\nUnlike some of the tax rioters, Hatoyama did not leave the DPJ to join [[Ichiro Ozawa]]'s [[People's Life First]] party, but continued to act within the DPJ to fight against both the consumption tax increase and the restart of nuclear plants. On 20 July 2012 he addressed a crowd of protesters outside the prime minister's residence, saying it was premature to restart nuclear reactors.<ref>{{Cite news|last=Kajimoto|first=Mari Saito, Tetsushi|date=2012-07-20|title=UPDATE 1-Ex-Japan PM joins anti-nuclear demo outside PM's office|language=zh|work=Reuters|url=https://cn.reuters.com/article/japan-nuclear-idUSL4E8IK2RD20120720|access-date=2020-11-23}}</ref>\n\nIn the lead-up to the 16 December 2012 general election the DPJ announced that it would not endorse candidates who did not agree to follow its current policies, including the consumption tax hike and support for joining the negotiations to enter the [[Trans-Pacific Partnership]]. On 21 November Hatoyama proclaimed that he would retire from politics.<ref>[[Daily Yomiuri]] [http://www.yomiuri.co.jp/dy/national/T121121004905.htm Former premier Hatoyama retires from politics 22 November 2012]</ref> Hatoyama was later expelled from the party.\n\nOn 9 January 2013, Hatoyama expressed a government apology to the victims of [[Japanese war crimes]] in China during a visit to [[Nanjing]]. He also urged the Japanese government to acknowledge the dispute between the two countries concerning sovereignty of the [[Diaoyu/Senkaku Islands]].<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.wantchinatimes.com/news-subclass-cnt.aspx?id=20130119000008&cid=1101 |title=Former Japanese PM Hatoyama apologizes for Nanjing Massacre |author=Staff Reporter|date=19 January 2013 |work=[[China Times]]|url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20141106003159/http://www.wantchinatimes.com/news-subclass-cnt.aspx?id=20130119000008&cid=1101 |archive-date=6 November 2014 }}</ref>\n\nOn 21 March 2013, Hatoyama was appointed as the honorary chairman and a senior consultant of [[Hoifu Energy Group]].<ref>{{cite press release|url=http://www.hoifuenergy.com/enewsinfo.html|title=APPOINTMENT OF HONORARY CHAIRMAN AND SENIOR CONSULTANT|publisher=Hoifu Energy Group|date=21 March 2013|access-date=21 March 2013}}</ref>{{primary source inline|date=December 2017}} The firm was named in the [[Paradise Papers]], despite the leak \"do not intend to suggest or imply that any people, companies or other entities included in the ICIJ Offshore Leaks Database have broken the law or otherwise acted improperly\", according to a disclaimer of [[International Consortium of Investigative Journalists]].\n\nIn March 2015, Hatoyama visited [[Crimea]] and claimed that the [[2014 Crimean status referendum|referendum in Crimea]] was \"constitutional.\"<ref>The Japan Times [http://www.japantimes.co.jp/news/2015/03/12/national/politics-diplomacy/ex-prime-minister-hatoyama-defends-referendum-crimea-constitutional/#. Ex-prime minister Hatoyama defends referendum in Crimea as constitutional]</ref>\n\nHatoyama practices the [[Transcendental Meditation]] technique and delivered the [[Maharishi University of Management]] commencement address on 23 May 2015 and will be awarded an honorary degree of Doctor of Laws.<ref>PRWeb [http://www.prweb.com/releases/2015/05/prweb12725731.htm Former Japan Prime Minister to Address Record Number of Grads at 2015 Maharishi University Commencement]</ref>\n\nHatoyama continues to display his outspokenness after his retirement from politics.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.ft.com/content/f0a23660-4918-11e6-b387-64ab0a67014c |title=Japan's ex-prime minister, Yukio Hatoyama, answers his critics |first=Robin |last=Harding |date=20 July 2016 |work=[[Financial Times]] }}</ref> He is one of the most followed Japanese current/ex-politicians on Twitter and still regularly attempts to weigh in on current affairs.\n\n==Awards and honors==\n\n=== Sustainable Development Leadership Award===\nOn 5 February 2010, Hatoyama was awarded the Sustainable Development Leadership Award of the [[Delhi Sustainable Development Summit]] 2010.
