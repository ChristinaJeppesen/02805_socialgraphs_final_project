According to legend, the character came into use during the [[War of 1812]] and may have been named for [[Samuel Wilson]]. The actual origin is obscure.<ref>{{cite book|last1=Schauffler|first1=Robert Haven|title=Flag day; its history|date=1912|page=[https://archive.org/details/flagdayitshistor00scha/page/145 145]|url=https://archive.org/details/flagdayitshistor00scha|publisher=New York : Moffat, Yard and Co}}</ref> \n\nSince the early 19th century, Uncle Sam has been a popular symbol of the U.S. government in [[American culture]] and a manifestation of [[Americanism (ideology)|patriotic]] emotion.<ref name=\"T.A. Hicks\">{{cite book|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=n7Inbt4923oC&q=Uncle+Sam|author=Terry Allan Hicks|title=Uncle Sam|publisher=Marshall Cavendish 2006, 40 pages|isbn=978-0761421375|access-date=August 1, 2015|year=2006|page=9|archive-date=August 14, 2021|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210814204920/https://books.google.com/books?id=n7Inbt4923oC&q=Uncle+Sam|url-status=live}}</ref> \n\nWhile the figure of Uncle Sam specifically represents the [[Federal government of the United States|government]], the female figure of [[Columbia (name)|Columbia]] represents the United States as a nation. An archaic character, [[Brother Jonathan]], was known to represent the American populace.\n\nUncle Sam has developed notoriety for his appearance in military [[Propaganda in the United States|propaganda]], popularized by a famous 1917 [[World War I]] recruiting poster by [[James Montgomery Flagg|J.M. Flagg]].<ref>{{Cite web|title=What's the deal with Uncle Sam?|url=https://www.thespectrum.com/story/life/2015/07/03/what-the-deal-with-uncle-sam/29669929/|access-date=September 27, 2020|archive-date=January 21, 2022|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20220121191345/https://www.thespectrum.com/story/life/2015/07/03/what-the-deal-with-uncle-sam/29669929/|url-status=live}}</ref>\n\nThe first reference to Uncle Sam in formal literature (as distinct from newspapers) was in the 1816 [[allegorical]] book ''[[The Adventures of Uncle Sam, in Search After His Lost Honor]]'' by [[Frederick Augustus Fidfaddy]], Esq.<ref name=AM>pp. 40\u201341 of Albert Matthews, \"Uncle Sam\". ([[Thirteen Colonies|pre-US]]) and sometimes was associated with another female personification, [[Liberty (goddess)|Lady Liberty]]. \n\nWith the [[American Revolutionary War]] of 1775 came [[Brother Jonathan]], a male personification. \n\nUncle Sam finally appeared after the [[War of 1812]].<ref>{{cite encyclopedia|title=Uncle (personification)|Columbia]] appeared with either [[Brother Jonathan]] or Uncle Sam, but her use declined as a national person in favor of Liberty, and she was effectively abandoned once she became the mascot of [[Columbia Pictures]] in the 1920s.\n\nA March 24, 1810, journal entry by Isaac Mayo (a midshipman in the [[US Navy]]) states:\n<blockquote>weighed anchor stood down the harbor, passed There was a requirement at the time for contractors to stamp their name and where the rations came from onto the food they were sending. Wilson's packages were labeled \"E.A.\u2014U.S.\" When someone asked what that stood for, a co-worker jokingly said, \"Elbert Anderson [the contractor] and Uncle Sam,\" referring to Wilson, though the ''U.S.'' actually stood for \"United States\".<ref>Wyandott Herald, Kansas City, August 17, 1882, p. 2</ref> \n\nDoubts have been raised as to the authenticity of this story, as the claim did not appear in print until 1842.<ref>{{cite web|work=Proceedings of the American Antiquarian Society, Volume 19|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=JQ4pAAAAYAAJ&q=%22samuel+wilson%22+%22the+most+popular+explanation%22&pg=PA47|title=Uncle Sam|author=Matthews, Albert|date=1908|access-date=July 2, 2015|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20151003080711/https://books.google.com/books?id=JQ4pAAAAYAAJ&pg=PA47&dq=%22samuel+wilson%22+%22the+most+popular+explanation%22&hl=en&sa=X&ei=dZqVVZ3ZEsPfsAXCgI-IBA&ved=0CB4Q6AEwAA#v=onepage&q=%22samuel%20wilson%22%20%22the%20most%20popular%20explanation%22&f=false|archive-date=October 3, 2015|url-status=live}}</ref> Additionally, the earliest known mention definitely referring to the metaphorical Uncle Sam is from 1810, predating Wilson's contract with the government.<ref name=\"newlight\" /> \n\n=== Development of the character ===\nIn 1835, [[Brother Jonathan]] made a reference to Uncle Sam, implying that they symbolized different things: Brother Jonathan was the country itself, while Uncle Sam was the government and its power.<ref>Morgan, Winifred (1988). ''An American icon: Brother Jonathan and American identity''. [[University of Delaware Press]]. pg. 81.</ref>\n\nA [[clockmaker]] in an 1849 comedic novel explains \"we call...the [[Americans|American public]] Uncle Sam, as you call the [[United Kingdom|British]] [[John Bull]].\"<ref>{{Cite book |last=Haliburton |first=Thomas Chandler |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=yJIlAAAAMAAJ&dq=%22American+public+Uncle+Sam,+as+you+call+the+British+John+Bull.%22&pg=PA20 |title=The Clockmaker, Or the Sayings and Doings of Sam. Slick of Slickville: To which is Added, the Bubbles of Canada, by the Same Author |date=1839 |publisher=Baudry |pages=20 |language=en}}</ref>\n\nBy the 1850s, the names [[Brother Jonathan]] and Uncle Sam were being used nearly interchangeably, to the point that images of what had previously been called \"Brother Jonathan\" were being called \"Uncle Sam\". Similarly, the appearance of both personifications varied wildly. For example, one depiction of Uncle Sam in 1860 showed him looking like [[Benjamin Franklin]],<ref>An appearance echoed in ''[[Harper's Weekly]]'', June 3, 1865 \"Checkmate\" political cartoon (Morgan, Winifred (1988) ''An American icon: Brother Jonathan and American identity'' University of Delaware Press pg 95)</ref> while a contemporaneous depiction of [[Brother Jonathan]]<ref>On page 32 of the January 11, 1862, edition ''Harper's Weekly.''</ref> looks more like the modern version of Uncle Sam, though without a [[goatee]].\n\nAn 1893 article in ''[[The Lutheran Witness]]'' claims Uncle Sam was simply another name for [[Brother Jonathan]]:<blockquote>When we meet him in politics we call him Uncle Sam; when we meet him in society we call him [[Brother Jonathan]]. Here of late Uncle Sam ''alias'' [[Brother Jonathan]] has been doing a powerful lot of complaining, hardly doing anything else. {{sic}}<ref>December 7, 1893 \"A Bit of Advice\" ''The Lutheran Witness'' he pg 100</ref></blockquote>\n\nUncle Sam did not get a standard appearance, even with the effective abandonment of Brother Jonathan near the end of the [[American Civil War]], until the well-known recruitment image of Uncle Sam was first created by [[James Montgomery Flagg]] during [[World War\u00a0I]]. The image was inspired by a British recruitment [[Lord Kitchener Wants You|poster]] showing [[Herbert Kitchener, 1st Earl Kitchener|Lord Kitchener]] in a similar pose.{{citation needed|date=December 2021}} It is this image more than any other that has influenced the modern appearance of Uncle Sam: an elderly white man with white hair and a [[goatee]], wearing a white [[top hat]] with white stars on a blue band, a blue [[tail coat]], and red-and-white-striped trousers.\n\nFlagg's depiction of Uncle Sam was shown publicly for the first time, according to some, on the cover of the magazine ''[[Leslie's Weekly]]'' on July&nbsp;6, 1916, with the caption \"What Are You Doing for Preparedness?\"<ref name=\"loc\" /><ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.livescience.com/mysteries/080630-uncle-sam.html | title=Who Created Uncle Sam? | publisher=Live Science | work=Life's Little Mysteries | access-date=February 16, 2012 | archive-date=December 3, 2008 | archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20081203113644/http://www.livescience.com/mysteries/080630-uncle-sam.html}}</ref> More than four&nbsp;million copies of this image were printed between 1917 and 1918. Flagg's image was also used extensively during [[World War&nbsp;II]], during which the US was codenamed \"Samland\" by the German intelligence agency [[Abwehr]].<ref>Macintyre, Ben. ''Operation Mincemeat'', p. 57. {{ISBN|978-1-4088-0921-1}}</ref> The term was central in the song \"[[The Yankee Doodle Boy]]\", which was featured in 1942 in the musical ''[[Yankee Doodle Dandy]]''.\n\nThere are two memorials to Uncle Sam, both of which commemorate the life of Samuel Wilson: the [[Uncle Sam Memorial Statue]] in [[Arlington, Massachusetts]], his birthplace; and a memorial near his long-term residence in Riverfront Park, [[Troy, New York]]. Wilson's boyhood home can still be visited in [[Mason, New Hampshire]]. Samuel Wilson died on July&nbsp;31, 1854, aged 87, and is buried in [[Oakwood Cemetery (Troy, New York)|Oakwood Cemetery]], Troy, New York.\n\nIn 1976, Uncle Sam was depicted in \"[[United States Bicentennial|Our Nation's 200th Birthday]], [[Invention of the telephone|The Telephone's 100th Birthday]]\" by [[Stanley Meltzoff]] for [[Bell System]].<ref>[https://www.jklmuseum.com/tag/stanley-meltzoff/ \"Stanley Meltzoff Archives: The 1976 Bell System Telephone Book Cover\"] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210813094451/https://www.jklmuseum.com/tag/stanley-meltzoff/ |date=August 13, 2021 }} [[JKL Museum of Telephony]] (December 19, 2015); retrieved March 16, 2021</ref>\n\nIn 1989, \"Uncle Sam Day\" became official. A Congressional joint resolution<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thomas.loc.gov/cgi-bin/bdquery/z?d100:HJ00626:@@@X|title=Bill Summary & Status \u2013 100th Congress (1987\u20131988) \u2013 H.J.RES.626 \u2013 All Congressional Actions \u2013 THOMAS (Library of Congress)|work=loc.gov|access-date=September 13, 2013|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20160705024845/http://thomas.loc.gov/cgi-bin/bdquery/z?d100:HJ00626:@@@X|archive-date=July 5, 2016|url-status=live}}</ref> designated September&nbsp;13, 1989, as \"Uncle Sam Day\", the birthday of [[Samuel Wilson]].
