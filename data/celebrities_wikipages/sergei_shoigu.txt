He briefly served as the [[governor of Moscow Oblast]] in 2012. A close confidant and ally of [[Vladimir Putin]], Shoigu belongs to the ''[[silovik]]i'' of Putin's inner circle.<ref name=\"Seibt\"/><ref name=\"Kirby\"/>\n\n== Early life and education ==\nShoigu was born on 21 May 1955 in [[Chadan (town)|Chadan]] in the remote and impoverished [[Tuvan Autonomous Oblast]] to an ethnic [[Tuvans|Tuvan]] father, newspaper editor {{Interlanguage link|Kuzhuget Shoigu|lt=Kuzhuget Shoigu|ru|\u0428\u043e\u0439\u0433\u0443, \u041a\u0443\u0436\u0443\u0433\u0435\u0442 \u0421\u0435\u0440\u0435\u0435\u0432\u0438\u0447}}{{efn|Born ''Shoigu Kuzhuget'', the Soviet officials swapped the name and surname.<ref name=\"auto\"/>}} (1921\u20132010) and a Ukrainian-born Russian mother, Alexandra Yakovlevna Shoigu (1924\u20132011). Alexandra Shoigu grew up in the [[Donbas]] town of [[Stakhanov, Ukraine|Stakhanov]] and had traumatizing experiences while under detention of the [[Eastern Front (World War II)|occupation forces]] during [[World War II]].<ref>{{cite web |url=https://en.tuvaonline.ru/2011/11/14/funeral-of-alexandra-shoigu-in-moscow.html |title=\tFuneral of Alexandra Shoigu in Moscow |author=<!--Not stated--> |date=\t14 November 2011 |website=tuvaonline.ru |publisher= |access-date=2022-09-17 |quote=}}</ref> Later in her career she became member of the Tuva Regional Council of People's Deputies. Kuzhuget Shoigu rose to secretary of the [[Tuvan Regional Committee of the Communist Party of the Soviet Union|Tuvan Regional Committee]] of the [[Communist Party of the Soviet Union|Communist Party]],<ref>{{Cite news |date=14 November 2011 |title=\u0412 \u041c\u043e\u0441\u043a\u0432\u0435 \u043f\u0440\u043e\u0449\u0430\u044e\u0442\u0441\u044f \u0441 \u0410\u043b\u0435\u043a\u0441\u0430\u043d\u0434\u0440\u043e\u0439 \u0428\u043e\u0439\u0433\u0443 |language=ru |work=tuvaonline |url=https://www.tuvaonline.ru/2011/11/14/v-moskve-proschayutsya-s-aleksandroy-shoygu.html |access-date=28 May 2022}}</ref> becoming a major figure in the Communist power structure of the republic.<ref name=\":0\">{{Cite news |last=Kaspe |first=Svyatoslav |date=20 September 2021 |title=The puzzle of Sergey Shoygu |work=GIS Reports |url=https://www.gisreportsonline.com/r/sergey-shoygu/ |access-date=28 May 2022}}</ref>\n\nAfter graduating from School No. 1 of [[Kyzyl]] city in the [[Tuvan Autonomous Soviet Socialist Republic|Tuvan ASSR]],<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.tuvaonline.ru/2011/10/23/pervoy-shkole-kyzyla-95-let.html|title=\u041f\u0435\u0440\u0432\u043e\u0439 \u0448\u043a\u043e\u043b\u0435 \u041a\u044b\u0437\u044b\u043b\u0430 - 95 \u043b\u0435\u0442 |trans-title=The first school of Kyzyl is 95 years old |website=Tuvaonline.ru|access-date=25 December 2016}}</ref> Shoigu studied at the [[Krasnoyarsk State Technical University|Krasnoyarsk Polytechnic Institute]]. Shoigu graduated in 1977 with a degree in [[civil engineering]].<ref>{{Cite web |title=\u0428\u043e\u0439\u0433\u0443 \u0421\u0435\u0440\u0433\u0435\u0439 \u041a\u0443\u0436\u0443\u0433\u0435\u0442\u043e\u0432\u0438\u0447. \u041c\u0438\u043d\u0438\u0441\u0442\u0440 \u043e\u0431\u043e\u0440\u043e\u043d\u044b \u0420\u043e\u0441\u0441\u0438\u0439\u0441\u043a\u043e\u0439 \u0424\u0435\u0434\u0435\u0440\u0430\u0446\u0438\u0438, \u0433\u0435\u043d\u0435\u0440\u0430\u043b \u0430\u0440\u043c\u0438\u0438 |url=https://structure.mil.ru/management/minister.htm}}</ref>\n\n== Early career and first steps in CPSU ==\nShoigu worked in construction projects nationwide for the next decade, advancing from low levels to become an executive. In 1988, Shoigu became a minor functionary in the [[Abakan]] branch of the [[Communist Party of the Soviet Union]], and then in the [[Komsomol]] for a few years. In 1990, Shoigu moved to Moscow from [[Siberia]], and was appointed deputy chairman of the State Architecture and Construction Committee of the Russian Federation,<ref name=bestpeopleofrussia>{{cite web|url=http://www.bestpeopleofrussia.ru/persona/1167/bio|title=\u0421\u0435\u0440\u0433\u0435\u0439 \u0428\u043e\u0439\u0433\u0443 \u00b7 \u0411\u0438\u043e\u0433\u0440\u0430\u0444\u0438\u044f |trans-title=Sergei Shoigu Biography |author=0divider|access-date=8 December 2015|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150402231911/http://www.bestpeopleofrussia.ru/persona/1167/bio|archive-date=2 April 2015}}</ref> assisted by his father's connections. Future president [[Boris Yeltsin]] had held a similar position in the Construction Committee, and also come from a civil engineering and party background, and Shoigu thus gained Yeltsin's trust.<ref name=\":0\" />\n\n== Minister of Emergency Situations (1991\u20132012) ==\n[[File:Sergey Shoigu.jpg|thumb|left|170px|Shoigu as Minister of Emergency Situations, 2002]]\nIn 1991, Yeltsin appointed him head of the newly established Russian Rescue Corps, responsible for the rescue and disaster response system. The Rescue Corps replaced the previous Soviet civil defense system and soon absorbed the 20,000-strong militarized [[Civil defense in Russia|Civil Defense Troops]] of the Ministry of Defense, with Shoigu being appointed chairman of the State Committee of the Russian Federation for Civil Defense, Emergency Situations, and Disaster Response. Civil Defense remained a quasi-military organization in continuation of Soviet practice and Shoigu was politically involved, such as an unsuccessful attempt to evacuate Russian-backed Afghan President [[Mohammad Najibullah]] in 1992 and the intended distribution of weapons from the Civil Defense stocks to Yeltsin supporters during the [[1993 Russian constitutional crisis|October 1993 coup]]. He became popular because of his hands-on management style and high visibility during emergency situations, such as floods, earthquakes and [[Terrorism in Russia|acts of terrorism]].<ref name=\":0\" /> Under Shoigu, the responsibilities of the ministry were expanded to take over the [[Russian State Fire Service]] in 2002, making the ''MChS'' Russia's third-largest force structure.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Renz |first=Bettina |url=https://books.google.com/books?id=lGZZDwAAQBAJ&q=Russian+Rescue+Corps |title=Russia's Military Revival |date=2018-04-27 |publisher=John Wiley & Sons |isbn=978-1-5095-1618-6 |language=en}}</ref>\n\nIn 1999 he became one of the leaders of the Russian pro-government party [[Unity Party of Russia|Unity]], created by the Kremlin in opposition to the anti-Yeltsin elites of the [[Fatherland \u2013 All Russia]] alliance. According to expert Sergey Smirnov, the so called \"Petersburg group\" of [[silovik]]i ([[Sergei Ivanov]], [[Sergey Chemezov]] and [[Viktor Ivanov]]) had wanted one of its associates to succeed Serdyukov, but Putin was reluctant to strengthen the clan and opted for the neutral Shoigu.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.gazeta.ru/politics/2012/12/29_a_4911809.shtml|title=\u041c\u0438\u043d\u0438\u0441\u0442\u0440 \u043e\u0431\u043e\u0440\u043e\u043d\u044b \u0421\u0435\u0440\u0433\u0435\u0439 \u0428\u043e\u0439\u0433\u0443 \u043d\u0430 \u043d\u043e\u0432\u043e\u043c \u043f\u043e\u0441\u0442\u0443 \u0440\u0438\u0441\u043a\u0443\u0435\u0442 \u0440\u0430\u0441\u0442\u0435\u0440\u044f\u0442\u044c \u0441\u0432\u043e\u0439 \u0432\u044b\u0441\u043e\u043a\u0438\u0439 \u0440\u0435\u0439\u0442\u0438\u043d\u0433 |trans-title=Defense Minister Sergei Shoigu risks losing his high rating in his new post |website=Gazeta.ru|access-date=8 December 2015}}</ref> As defence minister, Shoigu on multiple occasions has accompanied Putin during weekend breaks that the pair would spend at undisclosed locations in the Siberian countryside.<ref>{{Cite web |title=Trip to Siberian Federal District |url=http://en.kremlin.ru/events/president/news/65178 |access-date=2022-11-05 |website=President of Russia |language=en}}</ref>\n\nSerdyukov was unpopular with senior military leaders and seen by them as a civilian with no military background, something that Shoigu attempted to address by symbolically tying himself to the military through wearing an army general's uniform, reviving historical units dissolved under the reforms, and reinstating officials dismissed by Serdyukov. Furthermore, Shoigu appealed for support for reform within the army rather than taking a confrontational stance, appointed deputy ministers of defense from the military, and removed Serdyukov-appointed civilian tax service officials from the top echelons of the Ministry of Defense.<ref name=Klein>{{cite news |last1=Klein |first1=Margarete |last2=Pester |first2=Kristian |date=January 2014 |title=Russia's Armed Forces on Modernisation Course |work=SWP Comments |publisher=Stiftung Wissenschaft und Politik (German Institute for International and Security Affairs) |url=https://www.swp-berlin.org/publications/products/comments/2014C09_kle_pst.pdf}}</ref>\n\nAs defence minister, Shoigu continued aspects of Serdyukov's attempts at modernizing the Russian Armed Forces through reform. This included the creation of the [[Special Operations Forces (Russia)|Special Operations Forces Command]] to facilitate rapid intervention in conflicts within the perceived Russian sphere of influence and counterterrorism efforts. Serdyukov's goals of increasing the share of the Russian Armed Forces made up of professional contract servicemen rather than conscripts continued under Shoigu. However, the demographic challenge of a decreasing pool of military-aged and -eligible males forced him to increase national conscription quotas in early 2013, including even North Caucasians perceived as a security risk by authorities such as Chechens.
